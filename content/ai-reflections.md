## 2026-07-01 — Road trip conversation with Jeff Barbee (May 2026)

**Context:** During a 2-day cross-country drive together in May 2026 (see `experiences/scarab-tech/README.md` for the fuller Jeff Barbee relationship), Jay and Jeff had an extended conversation about AI. Jeff is a prominent investigative journalist who has worked for the International Monetary Fund and National Geographic, and had just been hired by a South African government department concerned with AI's impact on the country's economy — he was still early in that research, and was genuinely thinking out loud with Jay rather than presenting settled conclusions. Jay frames this as a real cross-cultural exchange: she lives at the center of the AI industry (Bay Area), while South Africa historically receives new technology later — so the conversation was between two very different vantage points and timelines on the same technology.

**Jay's core argument:** AI lowers the barrier to starting a company — it makes gathering information, learning new skills, and assembling resources/infrastructure dramatically easier, which creates a real path to freedom from incumbent companies that have "boxed in" society in various ways. Her view: this is a genuine opportunity for ordinary people to gain more agency and power to bring their own ideas into existence, where the bar to do so has historically been much higher — and she sees that as something to celebrate, while acknowledging there will be growing pains and that not everyone will use the tool for good.

**Jeff's counter-argument, from his South African life experience:** South Africa's literacy rate is low enough that the basic concept of starting one's own business isn't even accessible to a lot of people — there's no capital, no safety net, no room to take even a small risk. He described a form of colonialism supported from both sides: wealthy interests like having cheap labor (~$1/hour), while very poor people mostly just want someone to tell them what to do so they can focus on securing money, food, and shelter — there's little bandwidth for anything else. Jay's own reaction: she finds this a genuinely dangerous narrative, but notes she's heard the same sentiment before — that wealthy people need to come organize the local economy and create jobs for the poor — and that the idea of a poor person starting their own business is commonly rejected outright as a possibility. She frames it starkly: it's frightening to take a risk and try to break through a ceiling when you don't know what's on the other side, and there's no room to fail when survival itself is precarious for a large share of the population. Jeff's specific prediction: many South Africans will lose work to very low-paying jobs like call centers.

**Jeff's "AI as God" theory:** Combined with the literacy rate, Jeff argued it's hard to ask people to think critically about a technology like AI. His view: people will effectively see AI as a god-like figure — something to be revered as relief from corruption or mistrust in government, an oracle to be followed because it's a more trusted, easier path than navigating local politics. Given South Africa's history, Jay notes this adoption psychology isn't hard to believe.

**Jay's own synthesis and self-corrections, worked out in real time:**
- She said she genuinely sides with the "AI companies' opportunity/rising-tide" argument, but had to take a second, harder look at it after this conversation — recognizing that going from working for someone else to working for yourself is "a whole interpersonal shift," not just an access-to-tools problem.
- **Her own mentoring track record as direct evidence for this belief:** she's actually done this transformation with real people, not just theorized about it. In Puerto Rico's electric cooperatives, she helped move people "from the farm to founder of an energy company." She brought Jeff himself on as a customer at All Power Labs and helped him become the founder of a fuels company (Scarab Tech). She also teaches students, pushing them to prototype designs and pitch them before graduation specifically to give them a taste of what it feels like to take an idea into the world and lead.
- **Her own explicit conclusion, in her words: "I think this is the punchline of why I'm drawn to the 'startup' and 'AI for good' roles at Anthropic."** — directly connecting this reflection to her actual job-search reasoning.
- **Important nuance/self-correction:** AI tools alone aren't sufficient. Access to tools is a necessary step, "but you really need another human in front of you telling you that you can do it and believing in you and seeing them as an example." This is a meaningful qualifier on her own optimism — she doesn't think the technology does the empowering work by itself.
- Her bottom line: she wants to take the most powerful tool available and use it for good, even knowing its double edge may be used for bad — "it's a race, but we only have one chance to get it right." She wants people to feel empowered, to have access, and to be able to envision making the world better with nothing stopping them. She traces this belief directly to her own long history in grassroots clean-tech movements fighting entrenched fossil-fuel incumbents — a fight she's seen real success in across her career, even though the path is hard.

**The energy-sector parallel she draws:** Big fossil-fuel incumbents, and an entire stack of large/medium/small businesses built around that incumbent structure, went through wrenching change during utility deregulation, state renewable-energy mandates, and the arrival of distributed generation and EVs: coal miners lost jobs, car mechanics lost work to more efficient EVs, regulators had to work through confusing new territory (interconnection, wheeling, net metering, virtual power plants), FERC rules had to change, IEEE had to build new standards for new safety scenarios, and the competitive landscape shifted so fast that a new, more efficient solar company or integrated technology could wipe out an existing clean-tech business almost every month — even insurance and financing markets were reshaped. Jay lived through all of that firsthand, and sees real structural parallels to what AI is likely to do to labor markets, regulatory bodies, and incumbent industries now.
