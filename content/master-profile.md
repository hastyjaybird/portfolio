### 3. Puerto Rico energy transition (2017–2020+)

- **Situation:** Post-Hurricane Maria; new energy regulation; commercial portfolio resilience needs.
- **Task:** Microgrid development and policy navigation for PR clients — two distinct projects, not to be conflated.
- **Action (ACEPR):** Post-Maria, Puerto Rico endured the longest and most deadly power outage in U.S. history; funding for new ventures was extremely difficult to raise in that environment. PREB (Puerto Rico Energy Bureau) had just issued regulation allowing electric utility cooperatives to form and operate for the first time — when the opportunity broke open island-wide, co-founded ACEPR to organize the emerging industry (cooperatives + solar/battery installers) and capture the momentum. Projects spanned a **46 MW hydroelectric dam**, a **150-building mixed residential/commercial portfolio**, and **800 distributed homes in a mountainous community**. PREB was overwhelmed by the volume of entities needing certification/regulation; worked directly with PREB to streamline their certification process and hold public comment.
- **Action (IOGS):** Founded/led IOGS (Industrial Off-Grid Systems) — an ISO/virtual-IPP model for a **51-building, ~5,000,000 sq ft industrial park** portfolio (Guardiola Real Estate, backed by Remedy Ventures PR Fund I), representing 74% of all industrial real estate on the island. Contemplated **25.2 MW / 75.6 MWh** battery storage capacity sized for 3-day critical-load autonomy. Managed RFP process, EPC/architect/structural-engineer coordination, and PREPA utility account transitions for tenants. Personally built the project timeline and financial model, including the Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) tax compliance structure that let the renewable energy infrastructure serve as a tax write-off for the partners.
- **Also:** Lived in PR ~2 years.
- **Result (ACEPR — cohort projects, names withheld):**
  - **The hydroelectric dam cooperative** (rural central-mountain region, ~61,000 people across five municipalities) ended up pivoting away from its original hydro focus and building out a lot of solar instead — part of a broader plan that had contemplated 46 MW from 3 hydroelectric dams and up to 5 MW blocks of distributed solar + storage (~$17.5M per 5 MW block).
  - **A rural, low-income mountain community's solar cooperative got built** — the community had taken a direct hurricane hit and gone 7+ months without power. The project was designed for 2–2.5 MW of solar + battery storage serving 800+ households (~3,000 residents), ~$10M total development cost ($8M private debt/tax-credit equity, $2M sponsor equity), and became Puerto Rico's first certified renewable-energy electric utility cooperative.
  - The relationships built streamlining PREB's certification process led directly to an ongoing engagement: a former PREB commissioner started **MaxETA** as a contractor working directly for the PREB commissioners, and Jay has worked with MaxETA ever since — close to PREB's day-to-day decision-making, with oversight visibility across **LUMA, Genera, HydroCo, PREPA, third-party aggregators, electric utility cooperatives, and other service providers.**
  - **Note: project/community names are intentionally withheld from all materials — refer to them only by the generic descriptions above.**
- **Result (IOGS, confirmed by Jay 2026-06-30):** Reached full design and engineering — "shovel ready" — but not construction; the partners were still finalizing details on the QOZ/financing structure when Jay left the project.

### 6. NanoSulf — customer discovery and an honest limitation (2015–2018)

- **Situation:** Devinder Mahajan's Stony Brook research group (Institute of Gas Innovation and Technology, I-GIT, housed within the Advanced Energy Research and Technology Center/AERTC) had a promising nano-catalyst (oxidative sulfur removal + polishing bed with a bed-life sensor) for scrubbing H2S out of landfill/biogas streams down to <2ppm — a real technical problem, since untreated H2S corrodes equipment, triggers regulatory fines (~$37K/day), and blocks landfills from monetizing gas-to-energy projects.
- **Task:** As Entrepreneurial Lead (pre-seed, pre-launch), run customer discovery to validate market fit and pricing while the technical team (Mahajan as PI/TL, David Tonjes as Co-TL, researchers Lori Clark and Taejin Kim) developed the catalyst itself.
- **Action:** Personally conducted **99–100 customer interviews** across landfill gas operators (e.g., Brookhaven Landfill, Waste Management/Altamont), EPCs (Unitel Technologies, SCS Engineers), and consultants; built segmentation (landfill ~$90M/yr, wastewater/AD ~$1.3B, oil & gas ~$300B) and a subscription-based go-to-market/pricing model (PowerBridge NY accelerator, Cycle 3).
- **Result:** The market validation work was sound — clear segments, real pricing, a signed test-cell customer at Brookhaven Landfill — but the venture stalled in the pre-seed gap between bench validation and a funded field pilot. I moved on to other ventures (BoxPower, XPrize, ACEPR). **Correction (2026-07-01):** an earlier version of this narrative claimed the underlying science was later vindicated by "Mahajan's group winning an NSF SBIR Phase II (award #144238)." This was checked and is **false** — award #144238 belongs to an unrelated company (Intramicron, Inc., Auburn AL); a direct query of NSF's award database confirms Mahajan has never received an NSF SBIR Phase II award (his complete NSF history is 3 awards, none of them SBIR). That claim has been removed. **Follow-up research (2026-07-01) on what actually happened:** the Brookhaven feasibility study did happen (independently confirmed), but nothing in Mahajan's patents, publications, or grants shows the H2S/landfill-gas catalyst work continuing past ~2016–2018 — his research visibly pivoted to hydrogen-economy and methane/CO2-management topics instead (his only two NSF awards since are 2020 wastewater treatment, the CPWaterTech venture I also worked on, and 2022 methane-hydrogen separation). **This is still the honest-limitation story, just without the false vindication beat: I did the discovery work right, but a pre-seed venture I led didn't survive the funding gap to commercialization, and the technology line itself appears to have quietly ended rather than succeeding without me.**
