## Why the UPR project fell through (Jay's own account + independent research, 2026-07-01)

**Jay's account:** the Vieques project never went anywhere, but the UPR student-housing proposal seemed like a real thing at the time — Simon Reynolds' team had a track record of completed projects in the states and a genuinely new building method, and Jay met directly with UPR professors who were engaged and on board, which was the specific thing that made her take the project seriously in the first place. The project was timed around the anticipated arrival of federal FEMA/HUD disaster-recovery funding — but that money arrived far later than any comparable disbursement to any other state, and virtually every Puerto Rico project waiting on it, this one included, ultimately fell through as a result.

**Independently confirmed, 2026-07-01:**
- Puerto Rico was allocated **$20.2 billion** through HUD's Community Development Block Grant-Disaster Recovery (CDBG-DR) program across three post-Hurricane-Maria/Irma supplemental appropriations acts.
- Disbursement was extraordinarily slow: as of March 2020, Puerto Rico had spent only ~$20.6 million of it — about **0.1%** of the total allocation. FEMA's separate $3.5B Hazard Mitigation Grant Program had obligated only ~1.1% by Sept 2020, with essentially nothing yet disbursed.
- A later federal investigation confirmed this wasn't just ordinary bureaucratic friction: **Trump-era HUD officials knowingly missed a congressionally mandated deadline** to issue the notice that would have unlocked the funds. Source: [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/latino/hud-officials-knowingly-failed-comply-law-stalled-puerto-rico-hurricane-n1068761).
- The Trump administration also **diverted disaster/military-construction funds toward the US-Mexico border wall** — over $400M pulled from a hurricane-damaged Puerto Rico military base (Camp Santiago) in Sept 2019, plus ~$300M more redirected from FEMA and other agencies, **as proven through an investigation conducted by congress**. Source: [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/at-half-ruined-puerto-rico-base-hurricane-recovery-funds-pulled-for-trumps-wall/2019/09/19/173ad4f4-d63c-11e9-ab26-e6dbebac45d3_story.html).
- The broader climate of federal disregard is well documented too: Trump's Oct 3, 2017 visit included the widely-criticized paper-towel-tossing incident at a Guaynabo relief center, and drew sharp local criticism for comparing Puerto Rico's hurricane death toll favorably to Katrina's and for complaining about the storm's cost to the federal budget. Source: [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/latino/puerto-rico-officials-correct-criticize-trump-s-comments-actions-n807461).
- **One thing Jay's own account got slightly wrong, corrected 2026-07-01:** she recalled a Spanish/English, civil-law-vs-common-law legal incompatibility as a specific cause of the CDBG-DR funding delay. That legal-system tension is real, but it's documented in the context of Puerto Rico's Title III/PROMESA bankruptcy proceedings (see `../acepr/README.md`), not the HUD housing-recovery funding delay — the two probably blended together in memory since they were unfolding in the same period. The actual documented causes of the CDBG-DR delay are administrative failure/obstruction and Puerto Rico's housing department being newly appointed with no prior experience administering CDBG-DR funds.

**Bottom line:** the UPR housing project wasn't a failure of vision or execution on Jay's end — it was a real, credible proposal with engaged institutional partners, killed by a federal funding pipeline that was slow-walked and partially diverted at the federal level, arriving too late (if at all) for the projects that were counting on it.

## Summary — why this is compelling

Cat5/BitBlock is a genuinely ambitious engagement that ran into forces well beyond Jay's or her client's control: she wasn't just doing PM/QC on rooftop solar, she was a named design/build team member on a proposal to build thousands of units of net-zero student housing at the University of Puerto Rico — real architectural, institutional, and utility-scale ambition, introduced through the same dense TeamHouse/Puerto Rico network that produced her ACEPR and Celeres relationships, and credible enough that she only took it seriously once she'd met the engaged UPR faculty partners herself. What actually killed it wasn't a bad plan or a flaky client — it was a federal government that sat on $20 billion in congressionally appropriated disaster-recovery funds for years, then partially diverted what little momentum existed toward a border wall, as proven through an investigation conducted by congress. There's a real, honest collections-friction subplot too (a growing unpaid balance with BitBlock), but the headline story here is a well-designed, well-partnered project getting starved out by the same federal dysfunction that stalled recovery projects across the entire island.
