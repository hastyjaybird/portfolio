## Key facts (verified)

- Prepared reagents and blood samples for HIV, Hepatitis C, and West Nile Virus testing.
- Handled high volumes of biohazardous samples under strict FDA regulation and quick-turnaround demands.
- **Sample volume, resolved (2026-06-30):** 7,000 samples/shift × 3 shifts/day = **21,000 samples/day** facility-wide. Jay confirmed this figure directly, superseding the earlier LinkedIn-derived "10,000/shift × 3 = 30,000/day" estimate — use 21,000/day going forward in all materials.
- Member of a global research team formed in response to the Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD/vCJD) prion outbreak, preparing the lab for prion testing readiness at a time when no licensed blood test for the disease yet existed.
- **The lab was one of five American Red Cross National Testing Laboratories in the US**; Jay's was the west-coast lab, in Portland, OR on Airport Circle. Source: Jay's own 2011 email description (below).
- **Testing method, described in Jay's own words** (2011 email to Tito Jankowski, OpenPCR founder, who was interviewing PCR users for product research): samples were mixed with a primer carrying an iron tag and an active molecule; sample tubes were placed in magnetic sleeves so that positive (bound) samples adhered to the tube wall while the rest of the solution was washed away; the samples then went through standard PCR amplification steps, with tagged nucleotides; the final readout was optical/chemiluminescent — active tags exposed to an acid then a base in sequence would glow, and a machine read the light output. This is consistent with a magnetic-bead capture + PCR + chemiluminescent detection workflow (e.g., TMA/transcription-mediated amplification-style blood screening assays used by blood banks in this era).
- **Both manual and automated testing ran in parallel**, by Jay's own account: "We actually did it by hand, and there was a machine that was automated that could handle large volumes. We had to test about 7000 samples per shift so we did both at the same time." When asked by Tito why some were done by hand, her direct answer was cost — the lab could only afford a few of the expensive automated machines, not that manual processing was required by protocol.

## Why it mattered / values / character (2026-06-30, Jay's own words)

- **Humanitarian motivation:** "I'm a humanitarian at heart, I like helping people." Took biochemistry coursework, which is what qualified her for the role — the appeal wasn't just the science, it was that the science had direct public-health stakes (blood supply safety).
- **Technical self-assessment:** "I'm good at doing precision lab work, I'm very familiar and good with all of the equipment, and good at doing math and following detailed complex procedures." This is a first-person confirmation of the precision/rigor thread that runs through her whole career (lab work → materials science PhD → complex field deployments).
- **Networker by nature, not circumstance:** Confirmed the Tito Jankowski thread wasn't a one-off — "I'm a networker, I like to connect people because it makes my network stronger and valuable to myself and the people around me." Worth carrying this framing into other experience folders as a recurring character trait, not an isolated anecdote.
- **Depth of commitment to the CJD/prion project:** Volunteered for overtime and graveyard shifts (midnight–8am) for 6 months specifically to support the prion-readiness research project. The project was a **multinational coordinated research and readiness collaboration**, with regular meetings involving counterparts in African and European countries — i.e., this wasn't a local lab initiative, it was Jay participating in real international outbreak-preparedness coordination while still in an entry-level lab tech role. This is a strong, concrete example of taking on hard/unglamorous work (night shifts, half a year) in service of a mission she believed in.
