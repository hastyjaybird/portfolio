## Origin story `[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY]` (2026-07-04, per Jay)

Jay's path into APL started before APL existed as a company — and it traces back to Windward Education & Research Center (see `../windward/README.md`), where she was Lead Researcher on the Biomass2Methanol program under mentor Walt Patrick, an old chemist who already knew about gasifiers and needed help building them at Windward's educational farm (this was her first hands-on gasification work, and the same relationship that later sparked her interest in Devinder Mahajan — see `../phd-stony-brook/README.md`). In 2008, she was working at Windward near Hood River, OR, alongside a role at a biodiesel plant in Portland — and the 2008 financial crisis hit both: the biodiesel plant stopped paying her. Around the same time she heard about Jim Mason, who was building gasifiers in Berkeley and had a strong online presence. Mason ran a paid workshop where participants built their own gasifier from a kit; Jay's group drove down to **The Shipyard** (Mason's Berkeley warehouse — APL wasn't a company yet) and built one of his earliest gasifiers, then hauled it back to the Windward farm in Klickitat. **It didn't work.**

When she left the biodiesel job (and Windward), she took the train down to Berkeley and walked into the shop to ask if they needed help. The system still didn't work; she had a chemistry degree and had independently developed niche, hard-to-find technical knowledge of biomass gasification (see below) — exactly the gap Jim couldn't easily fill. She told him plainly that the 2008 crisis had put her out of a job and she had nowhere to live. He offered to let her live at the shop. She became, per the original reference title she'd used, **"Chemist-in-Residence"** of The Shipyard — living on-site, and also functioning as **co-host, doing real community-building work**: when people hung out at the shop after hours, she was the one running that social scene, which included personally teaching welding and machining skills at the shop (per Jay, 2026-07-04) — a separate, parallel instance of the same teaching pattern that also shows up around the same era at Bay Area Derailleurs (see `../bay-area-derailleurs/README.md`), not the same workshops. This is the earliest chapter in a recurring, decade-plus pattern — see the "Community Building & Third Spaces" category (`docs/full-professional-history-review-2026-07-04.md`), which also includes Sudo Room/Omni Commons, Noisebridge, TeamHouse, and Bay Area MakerFarm. APL was founded right around this same period. She spent a stretch commuting back and forth between Portland and Berkeley by train to close out her life in Portland, then officially shut everything down and moved to Berkeley at the start of 2009. She lived and breathed the company's inception, before it was ever incorporated as a business.

## The only STEM founder — technical leadership across the whole stack (2026-07-04, per Jay)

Of APL's founding group, Jay was **the only STEM founder**: Jim Mason came from an anthropology/engineering background, Bear Kaufmann from environmental science, Ariel Fisk-Vittori came from green building, and the rest of the founding team came from non-technical academic backgrounds. **Jay personally carried the chemistry, chemical engineering, materials science, and process engineering** that made the gasification technology itself work — she wasn't just a generalist early employee, she was the technical backbone of a team without another formally-trained scientist/engineer in it. **Note (2026-07-04, per Jay): don't name specific co-founders or characterize their individual education/credentials in any materials — keep this framed around Jay's own unique technical role, not other people's academic history.**

That technical ownership extended well beyond her own bench work: she **hired and managed mechanical engineers and design-for-manufacturing staff**, and — worth highlighting explicitly for Anthropic — **she managed and directed a team of software engineers building APL's custom PLC (programmable logic controller) firmware and custom control software in-house.** She was very directly involved in this software/controls work, not just overseeing it from a distance — a genuine hands-on hardware-plus-software systems leadership role, years before "full-stack hardware startup" was a common framing.

## Product Support: a deliberate strategic pivot, not a demotion `[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY]` (2026-07-04, per Jay)

As APL's customer base grew, incoming support calls and troubleshooting requests were seriously distracting the engineering team from R&D — a real operational problem. **Jay brought a strategy to the board herself**: build a dedicated, separate Product Support department, train it thoroughly, and transition her own role to majority-remote Product Support Director while she went to graduate school — a deliberate, self-designed plan, not something that happened to her.

She personally interviewed and hired a team of **8 product support technicians** (corrected 2026-07-04 — earlier "5" was from internal decks; Jay's own recollection is 8), training them across electronics, software, and mechanical troubleshooting, plus soft skills — customer communication, cultural sensitivity, and physical durability for field travel. She ran this team from New York, genuinely bi-coastal: **summers and Decembers at APL in Berkeley, semesters at Stony Brook.**

Beyond staffing the team, she built the underlying quality/reliability system from scratch — **independently confirmed in detail via real internal APL documents (2026-07-04):**

## Summary — why this is compelling

APL is Jay's deepest, longest-running technical story, and the "only STEM founder" framing is the key to it: while her co-founders came from anthropology, environmental science, green-building, and other non-technical backgrounds, she was the one who carried the actual chemistry, chemical engineering, and materials science that made a gasifier work — and then went on to hire and direct mechanical engineers and a custom PLC/software team on top of that, giving her genuine full-stack hardware-plus-software technical leadership years before that was a common startup pattern. The Product Support pivot is an equally strong story in a different register: when customer support started eating into engineering bandwidth, she didn't just get reassigned to fix it — she designed the reorg herself, pitched it to the board, hired and trained an 8-person team, and built a real reliability-engineering practice underneath it (MTTF-weighted redesign decisions, an 8D failure process, CE-certification documentation, an in-house QA/QC burn-in program) — all while running the team bi-coastal and finishing a PhD. It's a rare case of someone being both the technical founder and the person who later built the operational/quality infrastructure a scaling hardware company actually needs. **This is also the clearest instance of a pattern that recurs across Jay's career: she personally bridges customer/field adoption feedback into engineering decisions**, rather than letting that signal get lost between a support function and an R&D team that never talk to each other — see the cross-cutting note in `content/master-profile.md`'s Career Summary section for other examples (NanoSulf, CPWater Tech, Sol Solutions).
