## Key facts (verified)

- Built the in-house QA lab from scratch; managed production, QC/analysis, R&D.
- **ASTM certification, clarified 2026-07-01 (correction from earlier phrasing):** Jay did not personally get "the lab" ASTM-certified. She was responsible for the fuel hitting ASTM spec: ran preliminary in-house testing, and once product hit spec, sent it to a third-party lab for official certification — she managed that whole process and the outside-lab relationship. The in-house QA lab she built was real and hers, just not itself the certifying body.
- **Title resolved 2026-07-01:** confirmed by Jay as **Chief Technology Officer** — supersedes the earlier "Director of Operations" title used across files. Dates kept simple as 2008–2009 (not exact months) per Jay's preference.
- **Oregon Oils relationship, clarified 2026-07-01:** Oregon Oils was **both an investor in Portland Biodiesel and its feedstock supplier** — physically next door, piped directly through the wall. Jay tested the waste-oil quality on Oregon Oils' side before the facility purchased and piped it over, and personally managed that relationship closely (quality gate + investor relations combined).
- **Location confirmed independently (2026-07-01):** a June 2008 article (found via web search) places the facility on **Swan Island**, Portland, OR — "finishing construction and expected to pump 1.4 million gallons of biodiesel," with plans to run entirely on recycled vegetable oil.
- **Throughput confirmed precisely from Jay's own engineering work product (2026-07-01):** a process/mass-balance document she authored, dated Feb 28, 2008 (`pdfs/Process_Balance_1.0mgy_2008-02-28.pdf`, from Google Drive), models the plant at exactly **1,000,000 gallons/year** (2,857 gal/day, 179 gal/hour, 350 operating days/year, 16 hr/day), using a KOH-catalyzed transesterification process (yellow grease feedstock, methanol, KOH catalyst, decant/wash/polish/methanol-recovery train). This is strong primary-source confirmation of the "1M gal/yr" figure used across resume materials — it's not an approximation, it's the actual process design basis. (The 1.4M figure from the June 2008 article above is likely an early/expected-capacity estimate that didn't match the final 1.0 MGY design.)
- **"Largest 100% waste-oil facility" claim — decision (2026-07-01):** Jay recalled the facility being "the largest biodiesel from waste veggie oil in the US (or West Coast) at the time." Fact-check attempts found no third-party source confirming or denying this — the two candidate comparators either don't count (Imperium Renewables' 100M gal/yr plant ran on virgin canola/soybean oil, not waste oil, so it's not a valid comparator for a waste-oil-specific claim) or partially count but blend virgin oil in too (SeQuential Pacific Biodiesel, ~5M gal/yr in 2008, primarily used cooking oil but blended with some Oregon-grown canola — so not 100%-waste). **Decision: use the claim, sourced explicitly to Jay's own account, not as an independently-verified fact.** Basis: this was the team's genuine belief at the time and for years afterward within the industry — no competitor or industry contact ever stepped forward to contest it. Framing to use going forward: **"believed to be the largest 100%-waste-vegetable-oil biodiesel facility on the West Coast at the time"** — the "believed" qualifier and "100% waste-oil" scope should stay attached whenever this claim appears, rather than stating it as a flat, unqualified "largest" superlative.
- **Why Portland (character/motivation, confirmed by Jay 2026-07-01):** Jay moved to the West Coast, specifically Portland, while actively job-hunting in renewables — she'd heard Portland had one of the first city sustainability offices/departments in the country and wanted to be at the forefront of the emerging clean-tech movement. Her own words: "I was thirsty to immediately go into renewables as soon as I could. I hunted the opportunity down and shoved my way in." (Independent context: Portland's Office of Sustainable Development existed before merging with the city's Bureau of Planning in Jan 2009 to form today's Bureau of Planning and Sustainability — consistent with, though not proof of, Portland being an early mover nationally.)
- **Environmental motivation (Jay's own words, 2026-07-01):** "I was really concerned about the impact of virgin vegetable oil, especially the deforestation from oil palm and other energy crops. Definitely circular economy and waste reduction was important to me." This is a direct, specific values statement — not generic clean-tech interest, but a documented concern about feedstock-driven deforestation specifically.
- **Why she left, 2026-07-01:** The 2008 financial crash hit the business — the owner didn't disclose that investors were pulling out, and Jay worked six months without pay because the team believed things would recover. The money ultimately ran out. She believes the business restarted years later, after she had already left. This is a real hardship/resilience story worth having on file, even if it doesn't go in a resume bullet directly.
