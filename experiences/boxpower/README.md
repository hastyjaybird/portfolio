## Key facts (verified)

- Mobile, drag-drop/plug-play, grid-tied and off-grid containerized microgrids.
- In-region business development focus: Alaska, Puerto Rico, Hawaii.
- Cohort of Launch Alaska's Start-up Accelerator program. **Correction (2026-07-01): Jay was not the only person from Box Power in the program — other team members attended for specific sections but were tied up with other business operations. Jay was the only one who completed the entire program in full.** Through it she gained deep, firsthand knowledge of Alaska's energy market, which she describes as complex and unique (isolated microgrids, extreme logistics/climate constraints, diesel dependence, distinct utility/regulatory structure per community). This is a good example of Jay going deeper than her role strictly required, and it's the direct root of her longstanding Alaska energy work (this connects forward to the Eklutna Energy Plan work under 10Power years later).
- Concurrent with SkySource/XPrize Director of Engineering role (confirmed by Jay). **How she managed both (her own account, 2026-07-01):** it didn't feel like too much because both projects were funded, she's good at running a team and catching the technical details that keep things smooth, and — critically — the two workloads offset each other in time. On the XPrize side, a long upfront period of technical work/design (built on years of prior team knowledge) was followed by manufacturing and assembly work she categorically doesn't do herself, and she came back in for integration, testing, commissioning, running, and troubleshooting the system during the XPrize competition itself (which happens in a single sprint week). On the Box Power side, after strategy/design work, there was a procurement/build/shipping lag (also not her work) before she'd be sent on a 10-day installation-intensive trip. She describes her own work style as sprint-based and suited to complete immersion (travel, installs, design sprints), which is how she figured out how to run both simultaneously. She was already contemplating leaving APL around this time, but didn't want to miss the chance to compete in the XPrize.
- **Humacao, Puerto Rico humanitarian deployment, post-Hurricane Maria (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01):** Jay met two Puerto Rico community members at a conference alongside Box Power, who described being without power, water, or communications after Maria — cut off badly enough that the mainland wasn't grasping the true severity of the situation. They asked if Box Power could get a system to Puerto Rico quickly. The Box Power team agreed to do the work as charity, rapidly building one of their containerized microgrid systems for shipment and installation in **Humacao, PR**, one of the hardest-hit areas on the island. Jay traveled to PR and installed the system herself over roughly 10 days. **This trip is the direct origin of ACEPR — corrected 2026-07-01: Jay never actually left the island after this trip.** See `../acepr/README.md` for the corrected version of this story (she made the decision to stay before ever leaving San Juan, and simply never boarded her booked return flight — she was already working remotely with Box Power anyway, so staying didn't conflict with the job).
- **Why Alaska fascinated her enough to do the whole accelerator (her own account, 2026-07-01):** she was genuinely excited to learn the market in depth — Alaska has the highest concentration of microgrids and microgrid expertise in the country; its social structure is shaped by Alaska Native tribal villages and Alaska Native corporations and their relationship to oil dividends; climate change is transforming the landscape there faster than almost anywhere else in the world, with significant state money moving entire villages away from riverbanks collapsing due to permafrost loss; and she sees Alaska's adaptation challenges as something close to a preview of compromises other regions will eventually have to make as climate change progresses. On top of that, many remote villages need heating/electricity access under an extremely difficult climate and use case — a hard, high-stakes engineering problem she found compelling in its own right, not just as a box to check for the business development role.
- **Company scale independently re-verified (2026-07-01):** 40+ microgrid systems installed across the US and Puerto Rico, with 70 more in development with utility partners (PG&E, Liberty Utilities); funding reported between $6.67M and $19.4M depending on source, with a Series A as recently as Dec 2025. **Caveat: these figures reflect the company's current/broader state, spanning years well beyond Jay's Feb 2018–Jan 2019 tenure — don't attribute the full current scale to her specific window.** No independent web source separately confirms Jay's individual role/title/dates (expected for a COO/VP-level hire at a small startup — this kind of detail rarely appears in press).
- **Dates locked in: Feb 2018 – Jan 2019, per Jay (2026-06-30)** — she stayed on into January to wrap up loose ends after the Dec 2018 "Contract Termination" thread (found in Gmail, sent to angelo@boxpower.io). Supersedes both the old "late 2019" figure and the Wix site's "Aug 2018."

## Summary — why this is compelling

Box Power is the single year where Jay's capacity for parallel, high-stakes commitment is most visible, and where the highest-leverage decision of her career happened almost as a footnote. She ran business development for a hardware startup across three of the hardest energy markets in the world — Alaska, Puerto Rico, Hawaii — while simultaneously serving as Director of Engineering on an XPrize team, and made both work not through brute-force hours but through a specific, self-aware theory of how her own work style operates: she runs in sprints, she's good at the parts of a project that require judgment and troubleshooting rather than execution, and she deliberately timed her involvement in each project around the natural lulls (manufacturing, procurement, shipping) that didn't need her. That's a mature, practiced approach to managing concurrent high-stakes work, not lucky scheduling. She was also the only person at the company to complete Launch Alaska's accelerator in full — not because the job required it, but because she was genuinely fascinated by Alaska's energy market as a live preview of climate adaptation the rest of the world will eventually have to do. And then there's the Humacao story: a charity deployment to one of Puerto Rico's hardest-hit towns after Hurricane Maria, done as an act of goodwill with no strategic intent behind it — and standing at the airport, she read one regulatory news item and made a life-altering decision in real time, never boarding her flight home. Box Power is where you see both halves of her operating style at once: disciplined, sprint-based execution on committed work, and the instinct to drop everything the moment she recognizes a once-in-a-career opening.
