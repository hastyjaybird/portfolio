## Key facts (verified)

- Technology: nano-catalyst oxidative sulfur removal (OSR) reactor + polishing adsorbent bed with a bed-life sensor, reducing H2S from 100+ppm to <2ppm in landfill biogas.
- Technical team: Devinder Mahajan (PI/TL), David Tonjes (Co-TL), researchers Lori Clark and Taejin Kim, mentor Franco Borrello.
- Jay's role: **Entrepreneurial Lead — locked in as the title, per Jay (2026-06-30).** She asked whether "Founder" would be clearer; recommendation was no — unlike APL, NanoSulf's core IP/technical direction was Mahajan's from the start, so "Founder" would overclaim. "Entrepreneurial Lead" is also a recognized term of art in NSF's I-Corps program (Entrepreneurial Lead / Technical Lead / Mentor structure), documented in NSF paperwork and PowerBridgeNY records — defensible if checked. Fix for "confusing" is a one-line gloss on first use in any product: *"Entrepreneurial Lead (the customer-discovery and commercialization lead role in NSF's I-Corps structure)."* Disregard the "CEO / Entrepreneurial Lead" variant seen on the old Wix site. **Confirmed via Drive monthly reports: 99–100 customer interviews** (March 2017 report: "Total: 99") across landfill operators (Brookhaven Landfill, Waste Management/Altamont), EPCs (Unitel Technologies, SCS Engineers), and consultants.
- Market segments sized at the time: landfill ~$90M/yr, wastewater/AD ~$1.3B, oil & gas ~$300B (oil & gas explicitly "future steps: explore this archetype" — not yet pursued while Jay was EL).
- PowerBridge NY accelerator, Cycle 3.
- **Outcome (the intellectual-honesty story):** the venture stalled in the pre-seed gap between bench validation and a funded field pilot — Jay moved on to other ventures.
- **Correction, 2026-07-01 — a claim here was false and has been removed:** this README previously said "the science was vindicated afterward: Mahajan's group won an NSF SBIR Phase II (award #144238) continuing the same reactor concept." **Checked and confirmed false.** Award #144238 belongs to Intramicron, Inc. (Auburn, AL, PI Hongyun Yang) — an unrelated company. Its technical description happens to closely match NanoSulf's approach (OSR catalyst + polishing adsorbent bed + bed-life sensor, <5ppm H2S at >90% conversion), which is almost certainly why a prior session pattern-matched it to Mahajan — but there's no found connection between Intramicron and Mahajan or Stony Brook. A direct query of NSF's award database by PI name confirms Mahajan's complete NSF award history is exactly 3 awards, none of them SBIR: 2022 I-Corps (hydrogen separation), 2020 I-Corps (wastewater/CPWaterTech), 2008 IUCRC (bioenergy center). **He has never received an NSF SBIR Phase II award.** A summary of this finding is also saved as a README in the NanoSulf Google Drive folder so it isn't reintroduced later.
- **What actually happened afterward — resolved 2026-07-01 (no pivot-to-success, more like a quiet pivot away):** Independent search confirms the Brookhaven Landfill feasibility study did happen ("feasibility studies conducted at the Town of Brookhaven landfill" per a Stony Brook faculty summary), but nothing in Mahajan's patent record, publication record, or grant history shows any continuation of the H2S/landfill-gas catalyst work after ~2016–2018. His Google Scholar publication list shows a clear topical shift away from desulfurization toward hydrogen-economy and methane/CO2-management topics starting around 2018-2020. His only two NSF grants since NanoSulf are both I-Corps awards on different subjects entirely: 2020 wastewater treatment (the CPWaterTech venture, which Jay also worked on) and 2022 methane-hydrogen separation. His lab (I-GIT, which he now directs) currently describes its focus as green hydrogen and CH4/CO2 management — no mention of landfill gas or desulfurization. **Conclusion: the technology line appears to have quietly ended rather than being picked up by someone else or continuing without Jay — Mahajan's research program moved on to hydrogen and (again with Jay) wastewater treatment instead.**

## Why it mattered / values / character (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

- **NanoSulf and CPWaterTech were both Jay actively spinning technology out of Mahajan's lab** — not one-off assignments, but a repeated pattern of her seeking out early-stage, unproven lab science and pushing it toward commercialization. Her own framing: this reflects a genuine enthusiasm for "really hard early pioneer work" — the unglamorous, high-uncertainty stage between bench science and a fundable business, which most people avoid.
- **Mutual inspiration with Devinder Mahajan:** by Jay's account, she was the first of Mahajan's students/collaborators to have already founded a company (APL) before working with him, and he had never previously tried to commercialize his own lab technologies. She believes their working relationship went both ways — her track record and appetite for commercialization work helped inspire him, and his deep technical credibility gave her ventures real scientific grounding.
- **Possible connection to AERTC's Advanced Energy Incubator — Jay's belief, not independently confirmed:** Jay believes her and Mahajan's repeated spin-out attempts (NanoSulf, then CPWaterTech) helped inspire AERTC to formally launch an innovation/incubator program around that period, and recalls Mahajan may have served on its board. **Independently confirmed:** AERTC does run a real "Advanced Energy Incubator" program (a physical incubator space in the Stony Brook Research & Development Park, housing multiple energy startups), and Mahajan's **Institute of Gas Innovation and Technology (I-GIT)** was itself formally established in **2018** as a partnership between National Grid and AERTC, with Mahajan as director — timing that lines up with the tail end of NanoSulf and the start of CPWaterTech. **Not confirmed:** any causal link between Jay/Mahajan's specific ventures and the incubator's founding, or Mahajan's specific board membership on the incubator itself (as opposed to his directorship of I-GIT, which is confirmed). Worth treating as a plausible, good-faith belief rather than a citable fact until/unless a named source turns up.

## Summary — why this is compelling

NanoSulf is the origin point of a pattern that defines a whole stretch of Jay's career: she doesn't just consult on other people's technology, she goes looking for the hardest, least-proven science and volunteers to be the one who finds out whether the world actually wants it. A tenured professor's lab science, sitting in the gap between a bench-scale result and a fundable business, is one of the least glamorous places in the innovation pipeline to plant a flag — most people wait for validation before committing. Jay ran 99–100 real customer interviews with actual landfill operators and EPC firms, built real market segmentation, and landed a signed test-cell customer, and when the venture still didn't survive the pre-seed funding gap, she treated that as a legitimate, honest finding rather than a personal failure. What makes this case study genuinely rare, though, is what happened during this review: an earlier draft of the story had quietly attached a false "vindication" ending to it — a claim that the science was later validated by a specific federal grant — and rather than let that stand because it made for a better story, the record was corrected the moment it didn't check out, with the false claim traced, retracted, and permanently flagged so it can't resurface. That's not just a story about resilience in the face of failure; it's a live demonstration of exactly the intellectual honesty Jay says she values — applied to her own bio, under no external pressure to get it right, which is the only way that trait actually means anything.
