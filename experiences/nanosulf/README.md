## Key facts (verified)

- Technology: nano-catalyst oxidative sulfur removal (OSR) reactor + polishing adsorbent bed with a bed-life sensor, reducing H2S from 100+ppm to <2ppm in landfill biogas.
- Technical team: Devinder Mahajan (PI/TL), David Tonjes (Co-TL), researchers Lori Clark and Taejin Kim, mentor Franco Borrello.
- Jay's role: **Entrepreneurial Lead — locked in as the title, per Jay (2026-06-30).** She asked whether "Founder" would be clearer; recommendation was no — unlike APL, NanoSulf's core IP/technical direction was Mahajan's from the start, so "Founder" would overclaim. "Entrepreneurial Lead" is also a recognized term of art in NSF's I-Corps program (Entrepreneurial Lead / Technical Lead / Mentor structure), documented in NSF paperwork and PowerBridgeNY records — defensible if checked. Fix for "confusing" is a one-line gloss on first use in any product: *"Entrepreneurial Lead (the customer-discovery and commercialization lead role in NSF's I-Corps structure)."* Disregard the "CEO / Entrepreneurial Lead" variant seen on the old Wix site. **Confirmed via Drive monthly reports: 100+ customer interviews** (March 2017 report: "Total: 99," continuing afterward) across landfill operators (Brookhaven Landfill, Waste Management/Altamont), EPCs (Unitel Technologies, SCS Engineers), and consultants. Built market segmentation, business plan and pricing model and go-to-market strategy.
- Market segments sized at the time: landfill ~$90M/yr, wastewater/AD ~$1.3B, oil & gas ~$300B (oil & gas explicitly "future steps: explore this archetype" — not yet pursued while Jay was EL).
- PowerBridge NY accelerator (3rd Cohort).
- **`[RESOLVED 2026-07-04]` Linde/Waste Management Altamont facility visit, context confirmed by Jay:** she and Devinder Mahajan toured one of the first compressed-biogas liquid fueling stations at the Altamont landfill (Waste Management's facility, in partnership with Linde) — used to fuel Bay Area garbage trucks on landfill-gas-derived compressed biogas. Waste Management/Altamont was one of NanoSulf's customer-interview targets (see above); Jay and Mahajan went back a second time specifically to do an initial assessment of whether NanoSulf's H2S-scrubbing technology could help Altamont's operation once commercialized.
- **Honest limitation:** the venture stalled in the pre-seed gap between bench validation and a funded pilot — Jay moved on to other ventures.

## Why it mattered / values / character (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

- **NanoSulf and CPWaterTech were both Jay actively spinning technology out of Mahajan's lab** — not one-off assignments, but a repeated pattern of her seeking out early-stage, unproven lab science and pushing it toward commercialization. Her own framing: this reflects a genuine enthusiasm for "really hard early pioneer work" — the unglamorous, high-uncertainty stage between bench science and a fundable business, which most people avoid.
- **Mutual inspiration with Devinder Mahajan:** by Jay's account, she was the first of Mahajan's students/collaborators to have already founded a company (APL) before working with him, and he had never previously tried to commercialize his own lab technologies. She believes their working relationship went both ways — her track record and appetite for commercialization work helped inspire him, and his deep technical credibility gave her ventures real scientific grounding.
- **Possible connection to AERTC's Advanced Energy Incubator — Jay's belief, not independently confirmed:** Jay believes her and Mahajan's repeated spin-out attempts (NanoSulf, then CPWaterTech) helped inspire AERTC to formally launch an innovation/incubator program around that period, and recalls Mahajan may have served on its board. **Independently confirmed:** AERTC does run a real "Advanced Energy Incubator" program (a physical incubator space in the Stony Brook Research & Development Park, housing multiple energy startups), and Mahajan's **Institute of Gas Innovation and Technology (I-GIT)** was itself formally established in **2018** as a partnership between National Grid and AERTC, with Mahajan as director — timing that lines up with the tail end of NanoSulf and the start of CPWaterTech. **Not confirmed:** any causal link between Jay/Mahajan's specific ventures and the incubator's founding, or Mahajan's specific board membership on the incubator itself (as opposed to his directorship of I-GIT, which is confirmed). Worth treating as a plausible, good-faith belief rather than a citable fact until/unless a named source turns up.

## Summary — why this is compelling

NanoSulf is the origin point of a pattern that defines a whole stretch of Jay's career: she doesn't just consult on other people's technology, she goes looking for the hardest, least-proven science and volunteers to be the one who finds out whether the world actually wants it. A tenured professor's lab science, sitting in the gap between a bench-scale result and a fundable business, is one of the least glamorous places in the innovation pipeline to plant a flag — most people wait for validation before committing. Jay ran 100+ real customer interviews with actual landfill operators and EPC firms, built real market segmentation, and landed a signed test-cell customer, and when the venture still didn't survive the pre-seed funding gap, she treated that as a legitimate finding, not a personal failure.
