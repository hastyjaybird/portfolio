## Why it mattered / values / character (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

- **Why go broad after 8 years at APL, instead of another single-technology venture:** In her own words, the renewable/clean-tech industry was changing fast — new technologies becoming accessible, new financing opening up for electricity solutions. APL had found a real niche (farmers and green-waste recyclers with a biomass problem they couldn't landfill or open-burn — especially in California), and business was good, but she "wanted to go bigger" and "keep growing with the industry instead of getting stuck on something that seemed like a good idea 10 years ago." Advising across many different projects let her do a deep dive into many areas at once, specifically hunting for wherever a real, fast-moving break-point in the industry was happening — regulations in flux, some controversy, a need for education, a need for new ideas — somewhere with the potential to go bigger. Her own phrase for this, said candidly: she "was sort of shopping around" for the next big-impact area. This is a genuinely honest, self-aware account of deliberately searching for where to place a bigger bet, not just taking whatever consulting work came in.
- **The throughline skill across 15+ very different clients:** by her own account, her core repeatable strengths were technical due diligence (water, materials science/chemistry — biomaterials, fuels, solvents — and energy broadly: fuels, electricity, heating), consumer-facing strategy (UX, buying psychology, market strategy), team building and talent acquisition, technical grant writing, business/go-to-market strategy, and — her words, half-joking — "start-up founder therapy," since she'd already been through the founder experience herself at APL. That combination (deep technical rigor + genuine empathy for what founders are going through + go-to-market instinct) is a distinctive, hard-to-fake combination and worth naming explicitly in interview prep, not just implying through the client list.
- **Corvidae is the direct origin of the entire Puerto Rico chapter of Jay's career** — not a side effect, but a specific, dateable moment. See `../boxpower/README.md` and `../acepr/README.md` for the full origin story: a Box Power humanitarian deployment to Humacao, PR post-Hurricane Maria led directly, within minutes, to forging the idea for ACEPR at the airport on the way home. TeamHouse and Genmoji (both Corvidae clients) fed directly into that same PR network.

## Summary — why this is compelling

Corvidae is the clearest evidence that Jay didn't stumble into a broad, cross-domain skill set — she built it on purpose. After eight years going deep on one technology at APL, she made a deliberate, self-aware bet: rather than picking the next single venture and hoping it aged well, she'd advise across as many live, fast-moving corners of clean tech as she could find, specifically to locate where the next real break-point was happening. In her own words, she was "shopping around" — which sounds almost cavalier until you see the results: 15+ clients spanning hydrogen generation, plastic-to-fuel reactors, carbon accounting software, blockchain-financed housing, and an early-stage version of what's now a well-known carbon-removal company (Charm Industrial, advised during its pre-pivot, biomass-to-hydrogen phase). That range only works because of a genuinely rare combination underneath it: rigorous technical due diligence across water, materials, and energy chemistry, real consumer-behavior and go-to-market instincts, and — her own half-joking but accurate phrase — "start-up founder therapy," the credibility of having already been a founder herself. And Corvidae isn't a side chapter that happened to precede Puerto Rico — it's the literal mechanism that got her there: two of its clients, TeamHouse and Genmoji, are the same people and relationships that fed directly into the Box Power deployment and the founding of ACEPR. A consulting practice that was supposed to be a broad, exploratory bridge turned out to be the connective tissue for the biggest chapter of her career.
