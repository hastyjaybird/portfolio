## Values/character — why Celeres specifically (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

**Why she wanted this experience at all:** by this point in her career, Jay had already worked inside a nonprofit blood bank, a hardware/chemical company, an educational nonprofit (Windward), academia, a startup, and startup accelerators — but she'd never worked on the **venture capital** side or in the **public sector**, and VC in particular was a real blind spot. Her own reasoning: "I didn't know what it was like to be on that side of the table, and I thought it hindered my ability to negotiate or communicate effectively. There's no amount of training that's as effective as actually being one." The opening came through Puerto Rico's 2019 Bitcoin/crypto-migration era — she happened to already be on the island with a lot of secondary California connections, met Adam Krim through that network, and deliberately networked her way into working with Celeres specifically to get VC-side experience.

**Why due diligence over startup advisory, in her own words — a real ambivalence, not an unqualified endorsement:** she felt like she was making more impact per hour of work, because she was sitting closer to the lever controlling larger amounts of money — and for someone whose goal is scaling up impact, that felt like a form of scaling up. But she was candid about the tension: judging the startups and the technical people who'd worked so hard to bring something into the world "felt like judging from the sidelines. I wanted to be in the driver's seat." What she did enjoy: effectively getting paid to fly around and survey a wide swath of emerging technology to see what was likely to succeed — with the thought that she might use that vantage point to find her next thing to go work on directly. **Her own verdict:** "It seemed like the VC angle had a lot of on and off ramps... a good temporary vantage point for me, but I couldn't be happy about it long term. But I learned what I needed to. I now understand VCs." This is a notably self-aware, unromanticized account — she went in with a specific, bounded learning goal, got exactly what she needed, and knew from the start it wasn't where she wanted to stay.

## Summary — why this is compelling

Celeres is a genuinely different kind of credential from the rest of Jay's Corvidae portfolio: instead of being hired by a startup to help it succeed, she was hired by an investor to independently judge whether a startup's technology actually works — sitting on the other side of the table from the founder she's evaluating. The Blume Distillation brief shows this wasn't a rubber-stamp exercise: she got on a plane (well, drove — Berkeley to Watsonville and back) to physically inspect the biorefinery, interviewed both the founder and his equipment-manufacturing partner directly, and pushed back on a specific unverified purity claim rather than taking it at face value. For a firm evaluating hard-tech, physical-plant technology, that kind of "go look at the actual reactor" diligence is exactly the instinct you want from a technical reviewer — and it's a good complementary data point to the rest of her résumé: she isn't just someone who builds and pitches technology, she's also someone investors trust to independently vet someone else's. It's also worth noting who hired her: a team of career Goldman Sachs/JPMorgan/structured-finance professionals who, by their own backgrounds, had zero in-house ability to evaluate a biorefinery or a fuel cell — meaning Jay's technical credibility was literally the missing piece of their investment process for this asset class.
