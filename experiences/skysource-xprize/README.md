This single spreadsheet is strong, self-contained proof of hands-on engineering ownership, not just a managerial role.

## Why it mattered / values / character (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

- **System design, clarified:** WEDEW wasn't just "a gasifier plus an atmospheric water generator bolted together" — the genuinely novel part was combining moisture recovered from *drying the biomass feedstock before gasification* with water recovered from the *exhaust stream*, on top of standard atmospheric water harvesting. That combination let the system produce roughly **3x more water than a standard atmospheric water generator alone**. The design process itself, in Jay's words, was full of "really fun ah-ha moments" — the team knew early on they had something genuinely different, not just an incremental entry.

- **`[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY — per Jay's note 2026-07-01, keep this level of detail for interview prep, not the condensed long-form bio]` The generator crisis, during the competition itself:** Partway through the XPrize testing sprint (scores are averaged over the sprint period), the team realized their water output was coming in lower than expected. Investigation revealed the generator itself was damaged, severely derating the electrical output available to run the atmospheric water generator (AWG). Other team members kept trying to coax more performance out of the existing (damaged) setup — "still trying to milk the system," in Jay's words — but Jay recognized the team needed immediate, decisive action rather than incremental tuning, given that competition scoring was an average over time and every lost day compounded. She personally took inventory of every spare generator sitting in the workshop (old units, no budget to buy new ones), diagnosed each one's specific issues, and cannibalized/repaired/rewired parts across multiple broken units to assemble one fully functional replacement generator from scratch. She integrated it herself and got a second working system online in time to hit the required numbers. **Her own assessment, stated directly: "I single-handedly saved us and it was the reason we actually won the XPrize."**

- **`[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY — per Jay's note 2026-07-01, keep this level of detail for interview prep, not the condensed long-form bio]` The post-win IP lawsuit that killed commercialization:** The MOU's own admission (see below) that the team "didn't have the resources" to continue past the testing milestone undersells what actually happened. Skysource (APL's joint venture partner, responsible for the AWG side of the system) had hired an outside contractor who made modifications to the system and then, after the team won the $1.5M prize, sued Skysource claiming IP ownership over those contributions. The resulting legal dispute dragged on for **over a year**, and during that entire period the team was legally barred from using the XPrize win as marketing material, launching the product, or selling any systems — total paralysis on a technology with real commercial momentum. Jay's own words: "This really really upset me. I could see the technical and business path through, we had so much momentum and one person saw the $1.75M as 'a lot of money' even though it's definitely not for a hardware company. They were greedy and stopped everything single-handedly. Still to this day I'm super disappointed in it." She notes she already wanted to leave APL by this point regardless (she'd been there "too long"), but acknowledges the lawsuit removed any question of staying to see WEDEW through — "who knows, maybe I would have stayed" if it hadn't happened.

- **On managing XPrize + Box Power simultaneously (her own words, 2026-07-01):** "I was really familiar with the system that we did for the XPrize. My in-depth expertise made it easy for me to do both jobs. They weren't enough of a challenge for me to do simultaneously." Same underlying pattern as her general theory of parallel work: because she doesn't personally do procurement or manufacturing, whenever those activities dominated either project's timeline, it freed up bandwidth to run the other track in parallel.

## Summary — why this is compelling

The XPrize win makes a much better story once you know what actually happened underneath the headline. Most people, told their team's numbers were falling behind mid-competition, would escalate to whoever's in charge of hardware and wait. Jay's response to discovering a damaged, underperforming generator was to personally take inventory of every spare part in the shop, diagnose failure modes across multiple broken units, and assemble a working replacement generator from scratch under real time pressure — a hands-on, blue-collar-engineering instinct for solving a crisis with what's actually on hand, rather than what should be on hand. That's the difference between someone who manages an engineering team and someone who can also personally save it when things go wrong. And the story doesn't end at the trophy: a contractor dispute over IP ownership tied the team up in litigation for a year, blocking every path to commercializing a technology that had just won a global prize for solving a real water-scarcity problem. Jay's reaction to that — genuine, lasting disappointment rather than a shrug — says something honest about how much she actually cared about the mission versus the win itself. She didn't build WEDEW to collect an award; she built it to see the technology exist in the world, and watching that get strangled by one contractor's IP grab is still, by her own account, one of the more frustrating outcomes of her career.
