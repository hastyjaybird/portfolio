## Origin story — how Jay ended up in Mahajan's lab `[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY]` (2026-07-04, per Jay)

Jay didn't apply to a PhD program and land in Mahajan's lab by the usual path — she was recruited into it out of her own obsession. **The actual spark traces back even further than APL: Walt Patrick, her mentor at Windward Education & Research Center (see `../windward/README.md`), was the first person who ever mentioned Devinder Mahajan to her** — Walt was an old chemist himself, already excited about gas-to-liquids (GTL) conversion, and it was his enthusiasm that got her interested in the field in the first place. While at ALL Power Labs (pre-PhD), that initial spark grew into a genuine obsession — she researched GTL deeply, tracked everyone doing serious work in it, and even **built her own Fischer-Tropsch reactor prototype.** In the course of that research she found Devinder Mahajan's actual patents and came to see him as a genuine "rockstar" in the GTL space, reading everything he'd published; a chance secondhand account from someone who'd met him once ("he was delightful") only deepened the fascination.

APL regularly hosted workshops, and a colleague — without asking Jay first — sent Mahajan a genuinely embarrassing email along the lines of *"you have someone who really looks up to you at APL and you should come to our workshop."* He actually came out to see what APL was doing. During the visit, he asked Jay if she had a PhD; when she said no, he invited her to apply to the graduate program at Stony Brook on the spot to do gas-to-liquids research.

Jay often got recruitment offers like this from smaller universities that were APL customers — but this one was different in scale. She'd always intended to eventually go back for a master's, but APL had just started as a business; she asked Mahajan if she could defer a year, and he agreed. When the year was up, she realized she couldn't defer any further — her GRE score was about to expire and retaking it wasn't an option — so she took the opportunity.

**The relationship was genuinely mutual admiration, and it held for the entire program.** In her own words: *"we both really admired each other... it was such a good relationship throughout my entire program. I got so lucky, but luck didn't come by chance."* Mahajan could see immediately that she was a hands-on builder — she'd built APL, an actual hardware company, from scratch — so recruiting her wasn't just about her chemistry background; having her rebuild his lab from empty was, in her own framing, "definitely a favor to him." She backed that up fast: by the end of her first year she had built out the entire lab, was managing the most undergraduate students of anyone in the group, was the person other grad students called on to fix things instead of calling vendor tech support, and was generally the most useful person in the lab for helping everyone else's work move forward. She was also the oldest student in the group — everyone else was fresh out of undergrad, while she'd already started and run a company — which Jay describes as unusual for Mahajan to take on, but which became a real asset once she was there.

(Asked separately whether there's one specific moment/result/failure from the PhD that's the clearest throughline to her current climate-tech/hands-on-verification work: **her answer was no** — the throughline is really the whole pattern above, not a single moment.)
