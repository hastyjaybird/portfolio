## Key facts (verified)

- Technology: separates pure water from wastewater using a recyclable gas at low pressure/temperature, forming an ice-like solid that floats and is separated — no consumable chemicals.
- Team: Julia Hasty (EL), Devinder Mahajan (TL/PI), Kenneth Spall (Mentor).
- NSF I-Corps award **#2016164**, "A Non-Chemical Green Process to Treat Wastewater," ~$50K.
- Target segments: oil & gas (fracking wastewater), food & beverage (breweries), wastewater treatment plants/landfill leachate.
- **Customer interviews — corrected count (2026-07-01):** the actual PowerBridgeNY pitch deck states **"Total Customer Interviews: 68"** — supersedes the earlier "~20+" estimate.
- **NextGen CleanTech Funding Awardee** — PowerBridge NY (NYSERDA-funded) — this is the "NYS award" referenced elsewhere in materials.
- **NYSERDA EIR engagement, precisely documented (2026-07-01):** a signed Client Services Agreement between Columbia University and CPWaterTech (`pdfs/nyserda-eir-csa-signed.pdf`) confirms the engagement ran **May 7 – June 30, 2019**, for exactly **2.5 EIR days** — a short, focused mentoring engagement (not an extended residency). Columbia's assigned mentor was **John Wienecke**. Under the agreement, CPWaterTech committed to sharing non-proprietary business info with other regional companies/entrepreneurs and participating in NYSERDA/Columbia metrics surveys for up to 5 years post-engagement. **Notable: Jay remained connected to this network for years afterward** — Gmail shows NYSERDA EIR/Columbia "Skills Lab" webinar invitations as recently as 2023–2024 (topics: grant writing, investor readiness, HR/compliance, climate tech and AI with NVIDIA), meaning this wasn't a one-off 2019 engagement but an ongoing professional network relationship.
- **Timeline note, resolved 2026-07-01:** Jay initially described this 2019 Columbia engagement in the same breath as meeting Steve Blank and doing PhD MoS2-nanoparticle lab work — those actually belong to a separate, earlier Columbia stint (Jan–May 2013, CPaSS, under Prof. Somasundaran — see `../phd-stony-brook/README.md`). - **Market sizing, from Jay's own team's analysis (found 2026-07-01):** $271B total US wastewater treatment market; $29B oil & gas/fracking; $4.7B food & beverage; $1.6B breweries specifically; $65M realistic beachhead (regional breweries producing 20,000+ bbl/yr). Team calculated CPWaterTech's technology could offer a ~75% cost reduction vs. incumbent wastewater treatment (~$3/1,000 gal vs. ~$12.47/1,000 gal).

At that point, the IP resided with Stony Brook University, with the team in discussion with OTLIR to secure an exclusive license. **Could not confirm whether this patent application was ever granted** — searched two plausible-looking granted patents with similar titles/technology and both turned out to be unrelated (different inventors/assignees entirely — same trap as the NanoSulf/Intramicron mixup, avoided this time by checking inventor names directly). ## Why it mattered / values / character (Jay's own account, 2026-07-01)

- CPWaterTech was Jay's **second** spin-out from Devinder Mahajan's lab (after NanoSulf) — see `../nanosulf/README.md` for the fuller version of this story. Her own framing: NanoSulf and CPWaterTech together reflect a genuine, repeated enthusiasm for "really hard early pioneer work" — seeking out unproven lab science and pushing it toward commercialization, not a one-off assignment.
- Jay believes she was the first of Mahajan's students/collaborators to have already founded a company (APL) before working with him, and that he'd never previously tried to commercialize his own lab technologies — she sees the relationship as mutually inspiring rather than a standard advisor/entrepreneur-in-residence arrangement.
- She also believes this repeated pattern (with her) may have helped inspire AERTC to formally stand up its Advanced Energy Incubator program, and recalls Mahajan may have served on its board — **not independently confirmed** as causal, though AERTC's incubator program and Mahajan's directorship of the Institute of Gas Innovation and Technology (I-GIT, est. 2018) are both independently confirmed real. Treat the causal claim as a good-faith belief, not a citable fact.
- **On NanoSulf ending vs. CPWaterTech continuing — Jay considers both equally successful (her own words, 2026-07-01):** "I love the early work, the learning and the discovery and strategy process. Everything is worth trying." She went on to advise others in similar industries afterward, so the experience continued to be valuable to her regardless of the technology's own outcome. Her framing: she values "finding the truth" — if something didn't work, that's a legitimate, practical finding (not viable), not a failure of the process. She also holds that sometimes a technology is simply too early — "sometimes it's timing, and that's ok."
- **Why customer discovery specifically (her own words, 2026-07-01):** "I really really like customer discovery because it informs what a successful application looks like. You can't get anywhere without the customer." The NSF I-Corps grant put her on the ground, physically inside operators' facilities, interviewing them directly — she describes loving "the visceral and intimate experience looking at the details of customer adoption." She explicitly contrasts this with what she calls "armchair scientists" — people who project their own judgments and perspectives onto someone else's experience without ever having walked in their shoes, or who only operate at "the 40,000-foot view with no touch with on-the-ground reality." Her own mantra: **"think at both altitudes — 40k and 0ft."** She values curiosity and discovering what's actually happening on the ground. She also framed a broader legacy concern here: when you build something, it stays in the world forever, and she doesn't want her legacy to be "a bunch of piles of rust."

## Summary — why this is compelling

CPWaterTech is the clearest example in Jay's career of someone who separates her own success from the technology's success — and that distinction is exactly the kind of intellectual honesty that's hard to fake in an interview. She ran the same playbook twice on two different pieces of Devinder Mahajan's lab science (NanoSulf, then CPWaterTech): 68 real customer interviews inside actual brewery and industrial facilities, rigorous market sizing down to the beachhead segment, a genuine patent application, and a coherent go-to-market model — and she treats both outcomes as equally successful, because for her the win was the discovery process itself, not a specific commercial ending she couldn't fully control. That's a mature, differentiated answer to "tell me about a project that didn't pan out," and it's backed by a real philosophy rather than a rehearsed line: her stated aversion to "armchair scientists" who operate only at the 40,000-foot view, and her own mantra to "think at both altitudes — 40k and 0ft," describes someone who insists on ground-truth data before drawing conclusions, which is exactly the instinct you want in someone evaluating whether an AI system's outputs match reality. The fact that CPWaterTech's underlying hydrate-based technology quietly kept going at Stony Brook's Center for Clean Water Technology after she moved on — while NanoSulf's did not — gives her a real, lived data point on why some technologies survive the handoff and others don't, without her needing to take credit or blame for either outcome.
