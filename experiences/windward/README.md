## Origin story `[INTERVIEW-DEPTH STORY]` (2026-07-04, per Jay)

Jay met Walt Patrick at an Earth Day festival that got rained out — only the truly committed people stuck around, and she was one of them. Walt turned out to be an old chemist himself, and he already knew about gasifiers well before Jim Mason or APL existed; he needed help building gasifiers at Windward's educational farm. He was also genuinely excited about gas-to-liquids (GTL) conversion, and it was Walt who first sparked her interest in that specific field. **Walt is also the one who first told her about Devinder Mahajan** — the initial spark that eventually led her, years later, to deep-dive into Mahajan's patents, consider him a GTL "rockstar," and get recruited into his Stony Brook PhD program (see `../phd-stony-brook/README.md` for that full arc).

The real draw to Windward, in her own words, was finding "a hard tech sustainability nerd like myself" — specifically drawn to the Biomass2Methanol project itself, not the broader intentional-community lifestyle. Outside of her American Red Cross and Portland Biodiesel jobs, **this was her first hands-on work with gasification** — meaning the entire arc that follows (APL, the Shipyard, her PhD under Mahajan) traces back to this one rained-out Earth Day festival and an old chemist who happened to know about gasifiers before almost anyone else did.

Still active today at windward.org.

- **Actual role/title — "Lead Researcher" is a real title she used herself, alongside "Research Mentor & Instructor."** Her two most recent professional CVs (2015, 2017/2018) each list **two separate Windward entries**: one titled "Research Mentor & Instructor" and one titled "Lead Researcher," both May 2007–June 2008 — likely reflecting two facets of the same role (teaching/mentoring interns vs. leading the research program) rather than an error. She co-taught interns alongside Director Walt Patrick across integrated sustainable systems: aquaponics, earth-building/sustainable construction, solar technologies, sustainable forestry, gasifier design, and biofuel/biogas production from waste biomass.

In her own words (from a grant-application essay): he "oversaw the installation of eleven septic systems, more than a mile of water lines, some 2,000 amps of electrical service, and the construction of the various buildings" on Windward's 131-acre campus. ## Summary — why this is compelling

Windward is the real headwater of Jay's entire clean-tech/gasification career, not just a line on the resume before ALL Power Labs — a chance meeting with a fellow "hard tech sustainability nerd" at a rained-out Earth Day festival led directly to her first hands-on gasification work, her first exposure to gas-to-liquids chemistry, and the very first mention of Devinder Mahajan's name, years before she'd ever meet him. It's a clean demonstration of how she finds her way into technical fields: not through a formal program or a job posting, but by showing up in person for something niche and sticking around when everyone else left because of the rain. It also shows an early pattern of running two demanding jobs simultaneously (Red Cross lab + Windward) and personally owning a program end-to-end — grant writing, technical R&D, microcontroller programming, website maintenance — years before she'd call herself an entrepreneur.
