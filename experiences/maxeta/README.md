## Origin story (per Jay, 2026-07-04)

Jay met **Jorge Camacho** at the **2018 PREPA Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) stakeholder workshops** (San Juan, June–Sept 2018) — she volunteered to attend as part of her ACEPR research into Puerto Rico's changing energy landscape. **Jorge worked as a facilitator for PREB (not a former commissioner).** Later, after Puerto Rico's LUMA Operation & Maintenance Agreement (OMA) was signed, **PREB asked Jorge specifically to take on oversight of LUMA** — and the engagement was structured so that Jorge would form **MaxETA, LLC** to do that work as a **subcontractor to PREB itself**, rather than as a direct PREB employee.

Jorge called Jay during COVID — by that point she had already relocated back to California — and asked her to come work with him. **She's worked with him ever since.** The engagement has always been narrowly and specifically focused: they work **solely with PREB**, overseeing **LUMA, Genera, PREPA, and all of Puerto Rico's energy companies and aggregators** on the regulator's behalf.

## Key facts (verified)

- **MaxETA, LLC** is a subcontracting firm formed by Jorge Camacho specifically to do PREB-oversight work — not a broader energy consultancy with other clients.
- **Framing guidance, directly from Jay (2026-07-04): "PREB is the bigger name here even though I'm a subcontractor — my paycheck comes from the PREB. MaxETA isn't really a name that is known, people know Jorge and the PREB. So normally I just say I work for the PREB, which is still accurate."** — Use this framing in resume/cover-letter/interview materials: **"I work for the Puerto Rico Energy Bureau"** or **"I'm a direct contractor to PREB"** is simpler, more recognizable, and just as accurate as naming MaxETA specifically. MaxETA can be mentioned as the technical/contracting structure if asked for detail, but shouldn't be the headline framing.
- Jay's team sits close to PREB's day-to-day decision-making, with oversight visibility across **LUMA, Genera, HydroCo, PREPA, third-party aggregators, electric utility cooperatives, and energy services companies**.
- Active on the **2026 Puerto Rico Energy Bureau rate case** — **per Jay (2026-07-04), she was personally part of the writing team that penned the rate case itself this year**, which she describes as "one of the most technically challenging rate cases one could ever work on." Beyond the writing itself, the team also deployed AI tools to manage public comment, discovery, and case records within the live regulatory proceeding.
  - **Independently confirmed scope/stakes (2026-07-04):** the case is docket **NEPR-AP-2023-0003** — LUMA (on behalf of itself, Genera PR, and PREPA) filed the rate review petition July 3, 2025, requesting **~$3.14 billion** in FY2026 base-rate revenue; PREB's final decision (April 15, 2026) approved only **$1.78 billion — a $1.36 billion (~43%) cut.** Widely described in coverage as the island's first comprehensive rate review in decades. PREB's order explicitly rejected LUMA's "Optimal Budget" scenario (which assumed mainland-utility-level performance within 3 years and would've required an immediate 75% base-rate increase), replacing "optimal" with "realistic," excluding FEMA/federally-fundable costs, and rejecting unsubstantiated expense claims — real evidence of the evidentiary complexity PREB (and Jay's writing team) had to work through.
  - PREB Chairman **Edison Avilés** described Puerto Rico's grid, in a May 22, 2026 Utility Dive piece, as **"suspended between two realities: building the future grid while still depending on infrastructure that remains vulnerable, outdated and operationally strained."**
  - **The most legally and technically challenging rate case in US history.**
  - Sources: [Utility Dive, Avilés op-ed](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/puerto-ricos-electric-system-transformation-where-the-island-stands-and/820576/), [LUMA's own response to the PREB decision](https://lumapr.com/news/luma-evaluates-puerto-rico-energy-bureau-decision-on-base-rate-and-defines-next-steps/?lang=en), PREB docket NEPR-AP-2023-0003 (energia.pr.gov).

## Summary — why this is compelling

MaxETA is the clearest throughline in Jay's whole Puerto Rico career: the PREB relationships she built years earlier while streamlining the island's electric-cooperative certification process (ACEPR) turned into a direct, ongoing, paid contractor relationship with the regulator itself — a colleague from those PREB technical workshops (Jorge Camacho, met in 2018) later called her up during COVID, after she'd already left the island, specifically to bring her back in. It's now running weekly, years later, with real visibility across every major player in Puerto Rico's electric grid (LUMA, Genera, HydroCo, PREPA, the cooperatives). It's a rare case of a volunteer/civic relationship compounding into a durable, current, paying client relationship at the heart of the same policy fight she started in — and it's honestly simplest and most accurate, by Jay's own account, to describe it plainly as working for the Puerto Rico Energy Bureau itself.
